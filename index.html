Depression Is Not What
Most People Think It Is

It is not sadness. It is not weakness. It is a condition that reshapes how the brain processes motivation, reward, attention, and sleep — making everyday life profoundly harder than it looks from the outside.

Why This Project Exists

Depression affects over 280 million people worldwide, yet it remains one of the most misunderstood conditions in daily conversation. People often reduce it to "feeling sad" or assume that motivation and willpower can override it. This misunderstanding creates a gap — between what someone with depression actually experiences and what the people around them believe is happening.

This project exists to close that gap. Through five interconnected pages, we explore how depression manifests as specific, measurable symptoms; how those symptoms make ordinary tasks like studying, interviewing for jobs, sleeping, and maintaining friendships extraordinarily difficult; and what the biological and psychological research actually tells us about why these difficulties occur.

The goal is not to diagnose or treat, but to build genuine understanding — so that the next time you interact with someone experiencing depression, your response comes from knowledge rather than assumption.

A Note on Language

Throughout this project, we deliberately avoid framing depression as a disability or defining people by their condition. People experiencing depression are not fundamentally different from anyone else — they are dealing with a condition that alters how their brain and body function in ways that are now well-documented by neuroscience and psychology.

When we compare "typical experience" with "experience during depression," we are describing the effect of a condition on cognition and behavior — not drawing a line between two kinds of people. Anyone can develop depression. Understanding it is understanding something about being human.
